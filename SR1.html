GENESIS OF STRESS

Our ancestors did not have the luxury of shelter, protection, and constant supply of food. They had to find their own dwelling place to gain protection from the forces of nature, create weapons to protect themselves from predators, and hunt for food to fill their stomachs. Danger lurked anywhere and everywhere. In order to physically survive, they had to adapt and develop a defence mechanism that maximised their chances of survival. Such is the “fight and flight” mechanism. 

Over time, humans developed into civilisations. The concerns that our predecessors had, that is to physically survive, no longer apply in gross terms. However, what had helped our predecessors thrive was passed down the generations through inheritance of genetics. As our physical survival became less and less of a concern, the mechanism was channelled to other life events that our civilisation created. Such is the stress that we all experience. It is our body and mind reacting to any kind of demand or threat in our lives.

A lot of the times, stress comes from the mental events that are shaped by the life events we go through. We might get stressed over the thoughts and feelings of homework, relationships, social media, family fights etc. This is not necessarily a bad thing. Stress can be part of positive experiences, such as going on a date, starting a new job, and helping us get the better of ourselves. However, too much stress can be crippling and detrimental to our mental health. We start to feel a variety of things when we are stressed, including tension, anxiety, and low mood. Sometimes, we even experience physical symptoms such as headaches, muscle stiffness, increase or loss of appetite, or tiredness. Our mind and body are truly in distress.
